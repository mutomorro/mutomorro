Organisational root causes → customer impact
Teams work in silos, customers fall between them
Structural
No single team owns the full journey. Handoffs between departments create gaps where customers get lost or have to repeat themselves.
→ Drives 31% of failure demand. Worst at Application and Claims stages.
Front-line teams lack authority to resolve
Decision rights
Team members can identify the problem but need approval to fix it. Customers wait while decisions travel up and back down.
→ Average resolution time 3x longer than it needs to be.
Systems designed for the organisation, not the member
Operational
Internal processes and correspondence written for compliance, not clarity. Members cannot understand what is happening with their account.
→ 28% of contacts are "what does this letter mean?"
No feedback loop from front line to design
Learning
Front-line teams know exactly what frustrates members but have no channel to influence how services are designed. The same problems persist.
→ Known issues persist an average of 14 months before being addressed.
CX measured by satisfaction, not by experience
Measurement
The organisation tracks NPS and satisfaction scores but not the journey quality or failure demand that actually drives them. Fixing symptoms, not causes.
→ Scores improve temporarily after interventions then revert.
Designed service vs delivered service
How the service was designed
Single point of contact throughout
Clear, plain language at every stage

Proactive updates so members never chase
How members actually experience it
Passed between 3-4 people on average

The customer experience is an organisational challenge, not a service desk problem
Every root cause sits inside the organisation, not at the front line. Fixing CX means changing how the organisation works - its structure, decision rights, communication and learning loops. The front-line team is not the problem. The system is.
