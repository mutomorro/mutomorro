# Proposition Diagram Labels - Draft for Review

**Created:** 11 March 2026, 09:45 GMT
**Updated:** 11 March 2026, 10:15 GMT
**Purpose:** Get the words right before we build anything. Each diagram needs labels that make the visitor think "they speak my language."

**Principle:** These aren't consultant terms or textbook vocabulary. They're the words leaders actually use when they're describing what they're trying to achieve - or what's keeping them up at night.

---

## 1. Culture Change

**Visual:** Culture at the centre, conditions arranged around it.
**Caption:** Change the conditions, and culture shifts naturally.

**Centre label:** Culture
**Centre subtitle:** where energy flows

**Surrounding conditions:**
- How decisions get made
- How people collaborate
- What gets rewarded
- How knowledge moves
- What leaders pay attention to
- How change happens

**Why these words:** "Where energy flows" positions culture as something alive and dynamic, not a programme or a poster. The conditions around it are the levers leaders instinctively recognise.

## 2. Post-Merger Integration

**Visual:** Two overlapping circles with bright merge zone.
**Caption:** Integrate the whole system, not just the org chart.

**Left circle (Org A):** 
- Ways of working
- Decision-making
- Customer relationships
- Operational systems

**Right circle (Org B):**
- Culture and identity
- Talent and expertise
- Service delivery
- Knowledge and networks

**Overlap zone:** One integrated organisation

**Why these words:** Leaders navigating mergers worry about losing "ways of working" and "customer relationships" - not abstract constructs. The two sides deliberately use different language to reflect that each organisation brings different strengths.

## 3. Change Management

**Visual:** Landscape cross-section - straight "plan" line vs flowing line that follows terrain.
**Caption:** Change that works with the system, not against it.

**Straight line label:** The plan on paper

**Flowing line labels (terrain features it navigates):**
- Change readiness
- Stakeholder landscape
- Sponsor engagement
- Adoption barriers
- Team capacity and fatigue
- Where trust is strong
- Informal influence networks

**Why these words:** Mixes the practitioner vocabulary (change readiness, sponsor engagement, adoption barriers) with the human terrain (where trust is strong, team fatigue). Someone who's led change programmes will recognise both layers immediately.

## 4. Employee Experience

**Visual:** Surface touchpoints (small) above, deep organisational patterns (large) beneath.
**Caption:** When the organisation works well, the experience follows.

**Surface touchpoints (small, above the line):**
- Onboarding
- Surveys
- Benefits
- Events
- Wellbeing programmes
- Recognition schemes

**Deep patterns (large, below the line):**
- How managers actually manage
- Whether development leads anywhere
- How decisions affect people
- What happens when things go wrong
- Whether people feel heard
- How work is designed

**Why these words:** EX leaders know the frustration - they invest in the surface stuff (surveys, benefits, events) but the experience is actually shaped by what's underneath. "How managers actually manage" hits harder than "leadership capability." "Whether people feel heard" is what shows up in every engagement survey free-text box.

## 5. Organisational Restructuring

**Visual:** Triptych - current → transition → new structure.
**Caption:** Design the structure around how work needs to work.

**Left frame (Current):**
- Strained connections
- Workarounds
- Bottlenecks
- Unclear accountability

**Centre frame (Transition) - largest:**
- Knowledge being transferred
- Teams re-forming
- Relationships maintained
- Support in place

**Right frame (New):**
- Roles that make sense
- Work that flows
- Informal networks intact
- Room to grow

**Why these words:** "Workarounds" and "bottlenecks" are the everyday language of organisations that need restructuring. "Informal networks intact" is the thing everyone worries about but rarely sees in a restructuring plan. The centre frame being largest sends the message: the transition is where the real work happens.

## 7. Organisational Design

**Visual:** Five transparent layers slightly separated, bright glow where they align.
**Caption:** Design the whole organisation, not just the chart.

**Five layers:**
- Structure - spans, reporting lines, governance
- Operations - workflows, handoffs, operating model
- Collaboration - cross-functional working, decision rights
- Culture - behaviours, norms, what gets rewarded
- Capability - skills, capacity, development pathways

**Centre glow label:** An organisation that works

**Why these words:** Each layer now carries specific OD terminology (spans, decision rights, operating model) that signals expertise, while the plain-language subtitles keep it accessible. Someone commissioning org design will recognise this as a proper framework, not a generic diagram.

## 8. Organisational Purpose

**Visual:** Compass rose - purpose at centre, four directions.
**Caption:** Purpose that shapes decisions, not just decorates walls.

**Centre:** Purpose

**Four directions:**
- Strategy - where are we heading
- Culture - how do we behave
- Operations - how do we deliver
- Daily work - what people actually do

**Aligned nodes (glowing, close to centre):**
- Priorities reflect purpose
- Stories match reality
- People can explain why they're here

**Drifting nodes (dim, further out):**
- Purpose on the wall, not in the work
- Strategy disconnected from delivery
- New starters can't feel it

**Why these words:** Purpose leaders know the gap between "purpose on the wall" and "purpose in the work." The drifting nodes name exactly the symptoms they're trying to fix. "New starters can't feel it" is specific and human.

## 10. Organisational Capacity Building

**Visual:** Tree cross-section rings.
**Caption:** Build the organisation's capability, not just the individuals'.

**Rings from inside out:**
- Individual skills - what people can do
- Team capability - what teams deliver together
- Leadership capacity - how leaders develop others
- Organisational conditions - whether the system supports growth

**Arrows/connections showing:**
- Training alone doesn't reach the outer rings
- The outer ring is what makes everything else stick

**Why these words:** Capacity building commissioners often start by asking for training. These labels show that individual skills are just the inner ring - and the outer ring (conditions) is what they're actually missing. "Whether the system supports growth" names the thing nobody builds for.

**Caption:** Develop the whole organisation, not just the parts.

**Spiral passes through (labelled along the path):**
- Structure
- Operations
- Culture
- Capability

**Each loop labelled (inner to outer):**

**Caption:** Great customer experience starts inside the organisation.

**Inner layer (largest, most prominent):**

- Decision-making speed
- Front-line empowerment
- Escalation pathways
- Organisational culture

**Middle layer:**
- Service delivery processes
- Channel management
- CRM and systems
- Knowledge base
- Workforce capability

**Outer layer (what the customer sees):**

- Consistency of experience

**Left circle (User needs):**

- How they experience the service

**Right circle (Organisational capability):**
- What we can deliver
- How our systems work
- Where our expertise sits

**Overlap (brightest zone):**

## 14. Scaling Operations

**Visual:** Three nested outlines at different scales.
**Caption:** Grow the foundations, not just the headcount.

**Smallest outline (founding stage):**
- Close relationships
- Shared knowledge
- Flexible ways of working
- Strong culture

**Medium outline (growing pains):**
- Relationships stretched
- Knowledge stuck in heads
- Processes creaking
- Culture diluting

**Largest outline (scaled well):**
- Relationships redesigned
- Knowledge flows built
- Ways of working evolved
- Culture deepened

**Why these words:** Every scaling leader recognises "knowledge stuck in heads" and "processes creaking" - that's the growing pains stage they're living in. The largest outline shows the goal isn't to preserve the founding pattern but to redesign it. "Culture deepened" (not "maintained" or "preserved") is a braver promise.

---

## Notes and decisions

1. **Keep labels short** - max 690px display width means brevity wins. Short phrases, not sentences.
2. **Tone** - leader language as the base, with technical terms mixed in where they signal credibility (especially Change Mgmt, Org Design, Customer Experience).
3. **Density** - simpler is better at this display size. Some diagrams naturally need more labels than others and that's fine.
4. **"Not just" caption pattern** - fine across the set since people won't look at all 14 service pages.
5. **Culture centre** - "where energy flows" as subtitle.
